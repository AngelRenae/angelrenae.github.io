Specificity

  1. Home
  2. CSS
  3. Specificity

When applying styles in CSS, it’s possible to overwrite the style of one item when trying to edit another. To understand what will change when writing CSS, it’s important to understand specificity. To find the specificity amount of a selector, you create a number by multiplying however many id’s there are by 100, the amount of classes, attributes, or pseudo-classes by 10, and the amount of elements or pseudo-elements by 1. For example, the selector body#header.fixed-top would have a specificity of 111 since it has one element (body), one class (fixed-top), and one id (header). How specificity comes into play is that whichever selector that has the highest specificity, that one will be the final one applied. So if you had a selector that changed the color of one element with a specificity of 1 and another selector that changed the color of the element with a specificity of 10, then the color that would be displayed on the page would be that of the selector with a specificity of 10. In the end, since CSS styles are applied in the order they are written in the document, if two selectors have the same specificity then whichever one is closest to the bottom of the document would be the one applied. Understanding and applying specificity properly is important since it can save you a lot of headaches down the line when troubleshooting your CSS.
